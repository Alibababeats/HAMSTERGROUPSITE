Analysis

During our affinity mapping process, we clustered our initial research data into three primary themes regarding the engagement between young adults and their local government: Time, Accessibility, and Mental Health. We uncovered a distinct pattern where a large amount of time for young adults is allocated online, this often is characterized by doom scrolling and short attention spans which directly impacts their accessibility to reliable news and government resources. A significant pain point we identified is the fact that most young adults are finding their news through social media. The constant exposure to localized and international crises including school shooting concerns and current events like the war in Iran, severely impacts their mental health. There is a gap in providing information that is both easily accessible to high school and college students without contributing to mental health stressors or school overload.


These discoveries will directly guide the design of the BeHeard platform by highlighting the need for a streamlined, non-overwhelming user experience that respects users' limited time and attention spans. Moving forward with our research, we plan to investigate exactly which formats of civic information are least likely to trigger anxiety while still keeping young adults adequately informed. Our design approach will prioritize clear, accessible, and balanced content delivery to minimize the negative effects of social media algorithms. By addressing the intersection of time spent online and mental well-being, we aim to design a solution that bridges the accessibility gap, ensuring multiple voices are heard without fueling the doom-scrolling cycle.
